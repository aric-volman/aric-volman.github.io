# What this is
This post serves as both a manifesto and a reflection of my first year as an undergrad at UCSD. It serves as only one perspective of my individual experience in a small snapshot of time. I brought up East of Eden as an influence on my philosophy and worldview. No one knows what will happen from this point on, including myself. But I do know by induction that each step in life must originate from a prior step.

East of Eden is Steinbeck's masterpiece; it handles the question of free will vs. fate. I am not sure which one is more correct, but I do know that an individual has a massive amount of agency in their learning and curiosity. I assume that by applying the principle of induction, the answer must lie in the middle, and it must be a combination of fate (a general direction) and free will (personal choice). Perhaps we will never know.

## A note about the Math Crisis
Unfortunately, as of November 6th, 2025, our institution released a damaging report on math proficiency. I assume you have read it and are familiar on the topic.

A thought emerged in my head this year about the idea of a Prisoner's Dilemma. Why can't a rational system such as UC cooperate in order to alleviate this issue? Why is it every university/man to itself? In this way our institution broke as the first to report about this issue. You know it's bad when your cohort (Class of 2029) is discussed on the news.

This was inevitable. There is likely a way to fix it, or at least I hope.

Many of us hold this to be shameful. People tell me that trust in UCSD has been eroded. As a student in mathematics, this especially hits near-and-dear to my heart.

There is hope in a current petition to the Regents. It has been signed by hundreds of UC faculty in the last remaining days of May 2026. It offers a glimpse of hope that our genuine concerns can be heard by those in charge.

I learned several things about my environment (no pun intended):

- University requirements are complete BS. Young people do not need a climate change class, we already have too much to think about. However, I would strongly recommend PHYS 12 because it is taught from a futurist and optimistic standpoint.

All professors in this quarter are a tie. I would like to mention that one of my professors was a post-doc but was very excellent at teaching 20C. 20C was nerve-wracking, because it was my first foray into how math department exams look like.
Lowest grade: 20C, A- -> Largely due to midterm

During this time a magical non-descript Math-CS fairy told me one day to take MATH 109 in Winter...

## Winter Quarter, 2026

The first real quarter, and my first taste into the frustration that is CSE 11. The lectures and exams were great in CSE 11, but the assignments were long and confusing. Would still recommend CSE 11 with Ochoa (because it gets you used to the expectations in CSE). I also took MATH 109, which was quite challenging. To balance things out and give me perspective, I took COGS 1, which was very awesome and thought-provoking. Lastly, I took WCWP 10A, which was also thought-provoking.

I clearly remember obtaining a 63.75% on my midterm in 109. This was possibly the lowest grade I have gotten so far. However, via a lot of effort and determination, I managed to pull myself to an A- very fairly (no curve). 109 is one of those classes where math finally makes sense, things start to click, and grades start to improve, but only in the latter half of the class. If you have enough preparation and logical cleverness, you can succeed! It is also a class that displays the diversity and tapestry of the Math department. I interacted with every major, including the new Math-Bio major, and there were ECE and MAE students taking the class as well. Proof is always important to everyone!

I learned more things about my environment:

- The contrast between upper div math and CSE classes is extreme. The math department puts a lot of care in ensuring that we succeed. There are no incentives for students to bother their professors about research, no complex grading schemes (yet), no B.S., just pure fun.
- The Cogs department is more vast and wonderful than I thought. It was at this time I got interested in ideas surrounding behavior/animals/the evolutionary lens, with evolution being the most powerful explainer. The brain is a fascinating and mysterious place.
- There are professors who thankfully hold the line and uphold academic integrity. I have never been accused, but I have seen instances of it happening.
- Writing is more than writing novels and reading Steinbeck, it is mostly just philosophy in disguise
- Winter can get depressing very very quickly.
- Upper div classes are not scary if you approach them correctly

## Spring Quarter, 2026

Fast forward to right now. I thought a Python class would be nice so I am taking COGS 108 (Applied data science). I am also taking MATH 154, which is about graph theory. After CSE 11 is CSE 29, which is the new C programming class. I am also in Warren Writing 10B (with a new theme, the American Dream!). Note: I had credits for CSE 12.

Regret: not having bandwidth for MATH 180A

I am not sure if I will ever have a more awesome quarter than this, for several reasons. I felt more connected to my professors and TAs in all of my classes, and I felt very good that I was putting myself to use and prioritizing the prereqs for CSE 100 (Stupidly easy data structures class according to some, with stupidly hard requirements). The COGS 108 final project taught me more than what the class itself taught me. I learned about time-series techniques and how important DFT/FFT is (using a library, of course). We analyzed accelerometer data that was associated with certain human activities. 80% of the project effort went into data processing and proper sampling. It inspires me to learn more about algorithms, data structures, complex analysis, and a lot of other topics that are really useful. Thankfully, I can take classes on these (as a true math major)!!!

Graph theory is special, because it is so important and so accessible. In the beginning of the quarter, I sucked at strong induction, but now it makes a lot more sense (I heard from a math fairy once that graph theory is key to learning induction). What is shocking is that many theorems were only proved during the 20th century, and that the field is rather new. It is the most important math of the 20th century by far. The most useful part of graph theory is flows. It teaches you the maths of machine learning, and how to handle maximum and minimum arguments (minarg anyone?). It also delves into basic algorithm types, and the terminology makes it shockingly easier to learn new algorithms. Cool stuff and 109 + 154 is a better math track than the CSE department's discrete math classes (which is a shame).

CSE 29 is special too. However, the grading can be exhaustingly unfair and it is a class where you must 100% everything and pray that your homework doesn't nuke your grade. This is only unreasonable once you discover that there are two types of people: hand-waving (physics, graph theory, psychologists, philosophers, some evolutionists who write books, etc.) and precise (engineering, chemistry, the rest of math, hardcore economists, basically anyone else). There is a sort of a lost knowledge in handwaviness. Quick thinking, such as Fermi estimation, is a powerful skill, and allows you to perform Jedi mindtricks and shortcuts during graph theory lectures.

There is also a lost notion of implicit vs. explicit knowledge. I happen to be a very implicit person, who gets a feel of things and tries to get to the point. For example, I cannot recall any history facts for you, but when reminded, I can get a really good feel on my vast historical knowledge. Not everything must be explicit, and explicit can be harmful (in a sense that it forces you to be mechanical). However, explicit is the primary mode of the engineer and computer scientist.

### Skepticism, RL, Academic Questions, and other thoughts

This is also the quarter I got interested in academic questions and thinking. Not a particular topic per-se, but rather the questions at the heart of science. It has led me to darker thoughts and skepticism. I am now a little bit skeptical of reinforcement learning, after reading about Moravec's Paradox and the qualms of robotics. I have always been curious about robotics, but I don't see a point in even the most modern RL approaches. The lack of biological inspiration and plausibility, such as the use of reward function engineering, still has me bothered. This is in context of the brain's reward mechanism, which is vastly different (thanks COGS 2!!). The brain doesn't work with backprop. Rewards are not binary (but there are solutions to this). Rewards in the real brain are probably more complex than just a backprop.

It is worth for humanity to try, but will it lead to AGI? Is human-like AGI even remotely possible? I doubt that, since we seek precision and perfection. We seek embodiment and a replica of ourselves. Perhaps there is a solution good enough. Perhaps we are foolishly competing with the human body, which has incredible energy density, computation, and motor control abilities. The point I am trying to make is that there is an upper bound, a realistic one.

It is also worth to mention that economics is an important constraint. ANNs and backprop are our best bet with our current Von Neumann machines. Systolic arrays, although very famously fast at matrix multiplication and adding, are bottlenecked by memory speed on many edge devices, such as Google's Coral TPU, or HAILO's chips. We are at a brink of a memory disaster, as memory sells for a pretty penny nowadays (in the AI bubble). Memory, logistics, and "moving the groceries" in general are very inefficient for a task that is biologically parallel. I believe that spiking neural networks and new memristor technologies are key to overcoming this. However, from a graph theory perspective, true arbitrary graphs that are easy to train and contain cycles (infamously RNNs) are far away in terms of practicallity. Consider the challenge of making a reprogrammable chip that can emulate any arbitrary graph or at least a representation of one.

A promising area of research in robotics seems to be a small revival in Nouvelle AI (look it up) and insectoid robots. Impressive work from MIT shows that insectoid robots are fascinating things. I recently read another impressive paper detailing a bee-inspired navigation system ([https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10461-3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10461-3)). These things motivate me and give me hope. It is impressive that someone could attempt to replicate navigation of an animal, considering the mathematical dearth, time and prowess humans put into creating the "perfect" nav system.
