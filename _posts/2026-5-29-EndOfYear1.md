# What this is
This post serves as both a manifesto and a reflection of my first year as an undergrad at UCSD. It serves as only one perspective of my individual experience in a small snapshot of time. I brought up East of Eden as an influence on my philosophy and worldview. No one knows what will happen from this point on, including myself. But I do know by induction that each step in life must originate from a prior step.

East of Eden is Steinbeck's masterpiece; it handles the question of free will vs. fate. I am not sure which one is more correct, but I do know that an individual has a massive amount of agency in their learning and curiosity. I assume that by applying the principle of induction, the answer must lie in the middle, and it must be a combination of fate (a general direction) and free will (personal choice). Perhaps we will never know.

## Belief in fate is flawed

Disclaimer: These are my own, myopic views, and they are perhaps wrong. I am simply stating what I observe.

I would suggest reading this excellent explainer on the truth of personal blogging: [https://spinningvisions.com/2023/06/29/telling-the-truth-the-reality-of-having-a-personal-blog/](https://spinningvisions.com/2023/06/29/telling-the-truth-the-reality-of-having-a-personal-blog/)

It would rather be convenient to believe in fate and only fate. After all, why not entertain yourself with happy thoughts, a bright future, and endless green pastures? In a greedy algorithm view of life, seeking the most obvious, comfortable or immediate solution is seen as the best thing to do. It comes from our basic state of being - the psychological need for closure (NfC) is a fundamental concept to keep in mind. Many students foolishly believe that today's trends and technologies will last the test of time (in their careers). Many place their personal belief in specific jobs and fields. Although it may be dangerous to say the quiet part out loud, I wanted to at least list the three types of undergrad I have indirectly encountered:

1. AI Hype
2. Consulting
3. Premed (to a little tiny bit of extent, I admire their effort and rigor)
   
In no way do I demean or disrespect these types of people. A certain small percent genuinely have a passion in these areas and succeed. For example, if you have a passion in medicine and your father is a doctor, then certainly go for it. To those people, keep doing your best. But I am at most a bit concerned that 99% of the people chosing these paths are ill-informed or misled. I will not describe why I am concerned or what annoys me the most. But I do want to describe at least one common theme across the three categories.

## Shouldn't there be some rigor?

A common theme in at least the first two directions is a perceived lack of logical rigor and thought. For example, an AI person could focus on LLM engineering all day, only to struggle with explaining classical ML techniques or scoff at them. LLMs might not exist in 10 years or might evolve into something else. Consulting has a similar feel; generally, I've seen that GPA boosting, easy classes, and personability are the ingredients for a successful consultant. Consulting might become transformed or partially extirpated due to AI. We need AI engineers that know the history and tradition of their craft. We need consultants that can problem-solve through logically tricky scenarios. In both cases, real people who seriously consult or engineer AI systems have years of experience, several degrees, and a strong foundation.

I do believe that the premed track is very rigorous and very good for those who can survive. But the same feeling applies. It is somewhat known that some students chose to optimize their GPA or go for the easier degree, for example, which sounds troublesome at best.

**In all cases, you cannot blame the student for this.** The student is a cog in the machine, seeking certainty. Not everyone can afford the risk of thinking freely, which takes a lot of time and energy. Instead, we must acknowledge that current trends and hype always drive students towards the most elusive, stable path in their time. Bubbles, booms, and busts always happen and will continue to do so.

I refuse to believe entirely in fate, chosing to face a sea of uncertainty instead. A general direction provides some promise, but does not paint the full picture.

## A note about the Math Crisis
Unfortunately, as of November 6th, 2025, our institution released a damaging report on math proficiency. I assume you have read it and are familiar on the topic.

A thought emerged in my head this year about the idea of a Prisoner's Dilemma. Why can't a rational system such as UC cooperate in order to alleviate this issue? Why is it every university/man to itself? In this way our institution broke as the first to report about this issue. You know it's bad when your cohort (Class of 2029) is discussed on the news.

This was inevitable. There is likely a way to fix it, or at least I hope.

Many of us hold this to be shameful. People tell me that trust in UCSD has been eroded. As a student in mathematics, this especially hits near-and-dear to my heart.

There is hope in a current petition to the Regents, signed by hundreds of UC faculty in the last remaining days of May 2026. It offers a glimpse of hope that our genuine concerns can be heard.
