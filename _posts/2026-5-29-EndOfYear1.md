# What this is
This post serves as both a manifesto and a reflection of my first year as an undergrad at UCSD. It serves as only one perspective of my individual experience as a snapshot in time. I brought up East of Eden as an influence on my philosophy and worldview. No one knows what will happen from this point on, including myself. But I do know by induction that each step in life must originate from a prior step.

East of Eden is Steinbeck's masterpiece; it handles the question of free will vs. fate. I am not sure which one is more correct, but I do know that an individual has a massive amount of agency in their learning and curiosity. I assume that by applying the principle of induction, the answer must lie in the middle, and it must be a combination of fate (a general direction) and free will (personal choice). Perhaps we will never know.

## Belief in fate is flawed

It would rather be convenient to believe in fate and only fate. After all, why not entertain yourself with happy thoughts, a bright future, and endless green pastures? In a greedy algorithm view of life, seeking the most obvious, comfortable or immediate solution is seen as the best thing to do. It comes from our basic state of being - the psychological need for closure (NfC) is a fundamental concept to keep in mind. Many students foolishly believe that today's trends and technologies will last the test of time (in their careers). Many place their personal belief in specific jobs and fields. Although it may be dangerous to say the quiet part out loud, I wanted to at least list the three types of undergrad I have indirectly encountered and usually agree to disagree with:

1. AI Hype
2. Consulting
3. Premed (to a little tiny bit of extent)
   
In no way do I demean or disrespect these types of people. A certain small percent genuinely have a passion in these studies and succeed. For example, it is excellent to have a true passion for medicine and to continue with this path. To those people, keep doing your best. But I am concerned that 99% of the people chosing these paths are ill-informed or misled. I will not describe why I dislike these types or what annoys me the most. But I do want to describe at least one common theme across the three categories.

## What hath God wrought?

A common theme in at least the first two directions is a perceived lack of logical rigor and thought. For example, an AI person could focus on LLM engineering all day, only to struggle to explain classical ML techniques or scoff them. LLMs might not exist in 10 years or evolve into something else. Consulting has a similar feel; generally, I've seen that GPA boosting, easy classes, and personability are the ingredients for a successful consultant. Consulting might become transformed or partially extirpated due to AI. We need AI engineers that know the history and tradition of their craft. We need consultants that can problem-solve through logically tricky scenarios. In both cases, real people who seriously consult or engineer AI systems have years of experience, several degrees, and strong foundations.

I do believe that the premedical track is very rigorous and very good for those who can survive. But the same principles apply. It is somewhat known that some students chose to optimize their GPA or go for the easier degree, for example.

In all cases, you cannot blame the student for this fault. The student is a cog in the machine, seeking certainty. Not everyone can afford the risk of thinking freely or being their self. Instead, we must acknowledge that current trends and hype always drive students towards the most elusive path in their time. Bubbles, booms, and busts always happen and will continue to do so.

I refuse to believe entirely in fate, chosing to face a sea of uncertainty instead. A general direction provides some promise, but does not paint the full picture.
